\begin{abstract}

\textbf{Introduction: }Corporate greenhouse gas emissions predominantly occur through the value chain, classified as ``Scope 3'' emissions. These emissions arise indirectly as a consequence of the firm's activities, for example through supply chain operations and product use, making them difficult to measure and control. Moreover, accurately estimating Scope 3 emissions is complex but resource-intensive as they fall outside of a firm's direct oversight. While machine learning has been explored as a potential solution, previous efforts have failed to significantly outperform simpler estimation methods, leaving predictive accuracy low. This limitation underscores the need for more effective approaches to improve emission estimates, which are critical for both corporate sustainability goals and regulatory compliance.
\\\\
\textbf{Objective: }The objective of this paper is to investigate the challenges that machine learning methods face in accurately estimating Scope 3 emissions. Particular focus is given to understanding how data-related issues impact model performance, offering insights into the connection between data quality, feature selection, and predictive accuracy. 
\\\\
\textbf{Methods: }To address this objective, I employ an empirical approach that examines four critical dimensions of the modelling process: target variable selection, feature engineering, sample composition, and model choice. By systematic experimentation with different combinations of these elements, I identify both successful strategies and persistent challenges. Additionally, I conduct three complementary analyses to deepen my understanding of performance limitations. First, I examine some outliers produced by the even most accurate model. Second, I apply a materiality filter to highlight the trade-off between data quality and data coverage. Finally, I explore the constraints of category-level modelling, emphasising the need to aggregate Scope 3 emissions data to a sufficient level for more reliable predictions.
\\\\
\textbf{Results: }Scope 3 emissions are best estimated by: using emission intensities (that is, emissions divided by revenue); a core set of features; and (where sufficient data exists) by focusing solely on material disclosures. Each of these choices significantly improves predictive performance compared to the alternatives. In contrast to these unambiguous choices, no single model architecture clearly outperforms the others for all target variables, with machine learning models offering performance which is comparable to simpler models. Nevertheless, by filtering the data using material disclosures, my best Scope 3 model achieves superior predictive accuracy over the best model for Scopes 1 and 2. Furthermore, with respect to predictive accuracy, these results represent a substantial improvement over previous machine learning attempts to estimate Scope 3 emissions. 
\\\\
\textbf{Conclusion: }Overall, my findings suggest that improving Scope 3 emission estimates depends primarily on addressing limitations in data coverage and quality rather than refining model architecture. While enhancing data inputs leads to significant gains in predictive accuracy, the comparable performance of machine learning models to simpler models indicates that the core challenge in Scope 3 modelling is not complex interactions or non-linear relationships, which machine learning is typically suited to address. This highlights the need for improved data practices over more advanced modeling techniques when tackling Scope 3 estimation.   

  
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}

Almost every country in the world has committed to the Paris Agreement, a legally binding international treaty that aims to limit the global temperature increase above pre-industrial levels to 1.5 degrees Celsius: to achieve this, greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions must peak before 2025 and decline 43\% by 2030 \cite{unfcc2024}. Therefore, at the time of writing, we should have reached peak GHG emissions and must expect steep reductions to follow, especially in carbon dioxide which comprises most of the GHG mix \cite{Ritchie2023}. 
\\\\
For these ambitious targets to be achieved, firms must play a part to reduce their carbon emissions. As ``what gets measured, gets managed'', a necessary first step is for firms to account and report their GHG emissions. This need motivated the GHG Protocol Initiative, an international accounting and reporting standard for business \cite{ghgprotocol2004}. To assist firms, the Protocol provides a framework that divides emissions into three categories: Scope 1 (S1) encompasses a firm's direct emissions; Scope 2 (S2) are GHG emissions from purchased energy; and Scope 3 (S3) are all other indirect emissions from the value chain. 
\\\\
With the GHG Protocol steadily being adopted, over time the availability of GHG emissions data has improved. Nevertheless, significant problems remain with the coverage and accuracy of S3 data \cite{Papadopoulos2022}. Startlingly, while on average over 80\% of a firm's carbon footprint is from S3 emissions, only around half of firms report some S3 emissions data, and only 20\% of firms report material S3 emissions \cite{ftserussell2024}. To try to fill these gaps, attempts have been made to use machine learning (ML) techniques \cite{nguyen2021, Nguyenetal2023}. However, compared to naive baselines such as the sector median, these state-of-the-art approaches have shown only marginal improvements. This is also reflected by the practice of third-party providers, who often use simple models to impute missing S3 emissions. 
\\\\
The goal for this project is to understand why ML approaches struggle to improve on simple baselines and statistical methods for estimating S3 emissions. Specifically, I investigate how issues like data quality, feature selection, and sample composition influence model performance, offering insights into why machine learning fails to deliver a breakthrough in predictive accuracy. I begin in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Background} with providing the necessary background to the domain of S3 data, with a focus on data quality and coverage. After describing my sample construction in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Data}, in Chapter \ref{Chapter:ModellingApproach} I lay out my modelling strategy. This is a systematic, experimental approach to find what does and does not work. After getting the best results I can, presented in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Results}, in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Extensions} I consider where the models struggle and what alternatives there are. Using the lessons drawn from this, in Chapter \ref{Chapter:FutureApplications} I offer some suggestions for promising avenues of applied ML work in the S3 domain. Chapter \ref{Chapter:Conclusion} concludes.

Moreover, the growing regulatory landscape in data privacy and the responsible use of data also imposes restrictions. Both the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe and similar frameworks globally emphasise the need for clear accountability in how data is collected, processed, and shared, especially when it is used to predict outcomes with significant social or environmental impacts.

\section{Historical context}\label{sec:HistoricalContext}
Beginning in the 1800s, naturalists were puzzled to find signs of glacial activity in places too warm for ice sheets to form \cite{young1995}. Then, as the century progressed, some scientists discovered that certain gases such as carbon dioxide caused a ``greenhouse effect'' in their experiments \cite{Ekholm1901}; therefore, they hypothesised that these GHGs might affect the planet's temperature through trapping heat in the atmosphere \cite{Arrhenius1896}. Accordingly, they found it plausible that past climates could have been very different from our own \cite{foote1856}. 
\\\\
Consistent with this theory, in the twentieth century scientists observed rising global surface temperatures and steep increases in the concentrations of GHGs \cite{Sawyer1972}. Thus, today's mainstream scientific consensus is that the earth's recent climate change is mostly driven by human activity: as economic development increased combustion of fossil fuels, this has raised GHG levels in the atmosphere resulting in higher global temperatures \cite{IPCC2021, RS2020}. 
\\\\
Accompanying the growing weight of scientific evidence was political conviction that something must be done. Consequently, landmark international treaties were signed such as the 1997 Kyoto Protocol \cite{UN1997} and the 2015 Paris Agreement \cite{UNFCCC2020} which legally required countries to reduce their GHG emissions. Building on this scientific and political momentum around climate change, growing popular concern for the environment further catalysed domestic support for national targets such as Net Zero \cite{Poortinga2023}.

\section{Policy context}\label{sec:PolicyContext}
Accurate GHG emissions data are vital for meeting legal obligations and reduction targets, but this is not their only use. As this data also measures the demand for GHG emissions, price discovery is possible for emissions trading markets, meaning that the externalities are appropriately priced and so excess emissions are prevented. As firms disclose their carbon footprints, investors may also better hedge transition risk and follow their ethical preferences. Finally, as GHG emissions are ultimately an unwanted source of inefficiency, good GHG accounting helps identify opportunities for reducing waste. 
\\\\
In light of this, policy makers recognised the need for an international standard for corporate GHG accounting and reporting. Therefore, in 1998, the \href{https://ghgprotocol.org/about-us}{GHG Protocol Initiative} was launched with a mission to develop and promote the adoption of GHG reporting standards for businesses \cite{ghgprotocol2004}. Since then, the Protocol has increasingly been incorporated into global reporting standards including the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI 305, \cite{gri2016}), the International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS S2, \cite{ifrs2023}) and the Taskforce on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD, \cite{tcfd2021}). Through its adoption into legal frameworks and voluntary initiatives, the Protocol has become a popular framework for GHG emissions accounting.
\\\\
The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard divides a company's emissions into direct and indirect emissions. Direct emissions come from sources that are owned or controlled by the company. On the other hand, while indirect emissions are a consequence of company activity, they occur at sources outside of its ownership or control. To help delineate this further, three ``scopes'' are defined for accounting and reporting purposes. First,  scope 1 (S1) emissions are direct emissions from sources which are owned or controlled by the company. Second, scope 2 (S2) emissions are indirect emissions from the generation of energy that is purchased, such as from electricity, steam, heat and cooling. Finally, scope 3 (S3) comprises all other indirect emissions that occur in the company's value chain. Together, these scopes encompass the total emissions footprint of a company summarised in Figure \ref{fig:Scope3Diagram} below.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{scope\_3\_diagram.png}
    \caption{Overview of GHG Protocol scopes and emissions across the value chain, \cite{ghgscope32013}}
	\label{fig:Scope3Diagram}
\end{figure}

In accordance with the Protocol, many jurisdictions require (or will soon require) S1 and S2 disclosures for publicly listed firms, including in the EU \cite{eu20232772}, the US \cite{sec2024}, the UK \cite{ukleg2013} and Japan \cite{fsa2022}. Furthermore, although S3 reporting is currently optional in most of these key financial markets, the regulatory landscape is shifting away from voluntary efforts and towards mandatory S3 disclosures \cite{ftserussell2024}. 
\\ \\
To illustrate these developments, I focus on how reporting requirements in the UK, one of the most progressive jurisdictions in climate reporting, have picked up over time. Beginning in 2013, listed companies were required to report their S1 and S2 emissions \cite{ukleg2013}. To bring in additional reporting requirements for listed companies, in 2018 the UK introduced the Streamlined Energy and Carbon Reporting (SECR) regime. More significantly, this legislation also brought in new requirements for large unquoted companies and limited liability partnerships to report their S1 and S2 emissions. In addition, it strongly encouraged S3 reporting from all companies, signalling the intended direction of travel \cite{UKSECRGuidance2019}. Accordingly, the UK Government's stated ambition is to introduce mandatory S3 reporting across the entire economy by 2025 in line with the recommendations of the TCFD \cite{ukgov2020}. 

\section{Scope 3 reporting} \label{sec:Scope3Reporting}
Having set out the origins and development of GHG emission targets, it remains to consider what is required for a firm to comply with the GHG Protocol's  \textit{Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Accounting and Reporting Standard} \cite{ghgscope32013}. Possessing a solid understanding of this process will help illuminate the issues of data quality which are covered in Section \ref{sec:Scope3Challenges}.\footnote{S1 and S2 reporting is relatively straightforward as it follows from data within a firm's purview. Accordingly, S3 is where most effort on data coverage, consistency, quality and modelling is required \cite{ManGroup2022}, \cite{Papadopoulos2022}, \cite{ftserussell2024}.}
\\ \\
After the firm has divided its GHG emissions into direct and indirect, the first task is to set the S3 boundary; in short, this means accounting for all S3 emissions in the value chain and to disclose and justify any exclusions. This includes the gases contained in the Kyoto Protocol which are carbon dioxide (C0\textsubscript{2}), methane (CH\textsubscript{4}), nitrous oxide (N\textsubscript{2}0), hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), perfluorocarbons (PFCs) and lastly sulphur hexafluoride (SF\textsubscript{6}). Furthermore, the company is required to account for these emissions in their respective S3 categories as listed in Table \ref{tab:Scope3Categories}.\\

These fifteen categories are designed to help firms organise, understand and report the diversity of S3 activities in their value chain. Though aiming for completeness, clearly in some cases accounting for every category will not be feasible.  Therefore, the Protocol concedes that while some omissions might be permitted, ``companies should ensure that the S3 inventory appropriately reflects the GHG emissions of the company ... In particular, companies should not exclude any activity that is expected to contribute significantly to the company's total S3 emissions'' \cite{ghgscope32013}. It remains for the company to judge what is ``appropriate'' and ``signficant'' to report. 
\\ \\
After identifying and categorising the most likely sources of significant emissions in the value chain, the firm must then collect data for quantifying these emissions. There are two main methods to do this. First, where the firm has access to direct emissions data, they can simply use direct measurement. In practice, the second method of calculation is much more common, in which the firm uses activity data and emission factors to estimate emissions. In any case, the actual or estimated emissions data is then converted into a carbon dioxide equivalent (C0\textsubscript{2}e) so that S3 emissions can be aggregated and compared regardless of GHG type. If any needed data remains missing, firms may use proxy data to fill gaps. 
\\ \\
Using this data, the firm must next estimate their S3 emissions for each category. Importantly, companies may use a combination of calculation methods both within and across categories.\footnote{Note, therefore, that the firm is expected to collect data first and then, based on what is available, apply the most appropriate method for each category, and not the other way around.} There are three calculation methods outlined in the GHG Protocol's S3 guidance and recognised by the literature \cite{ghgscope32013, GoldhammerEtAl2017}. First, there is a process analysis (PA) approach which is bottom-up: it uses primary and secondary production process data and its associated carbon emissions. This has a high level of specificity and focus but may be costly or require data that is not possible to collect. Second, there is input-output analysis (IOA) which is top-down: it uses the input-output tables in the national accounts to allocate the carbon emissions of the economy to smaller units on a sectoral basis. This approach is simple to apply but by providing only averages in a population, it lacks nuance and it is difficult to show the results of reduction efforts. Third, there are hybrid approaches which combine PA and IOA to capture the strengths of both: under hybrid approaches, companies usually begin bottom-up and then fill any remaining gaps top-down \cite{Crawford2008}. 
\\ \\
Finally, to comply with the GHG Protocol S3 Standard \cite{ghgscope32013}, the firm must report their S3 emissions with the following requirements:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Total scope emissions reported separately by each S3 category;
	\item For each S3 category, total GHG emissions reported in metric tonnes of C0\textsubscript{2} equivalent, excluding biogenic C0\textsubscript{2} emissions and independent of any GHG trades, such as purchases, sales, or transfers of offsets or allowances;
	\item A list of S3 categories and activities included in the inventory;
	\item A list of S3 categories or activities excluded from the inventory, and each with justification for their exclusion;
	\item Once a base year has been established: the year chosen as the S3 base year; the rationale for choosing the base year; the base year's recalculation policy; S3 emissions by category in the base year, consistent with the recalculation policy; and appropriate context for any significant emissions changes that triggered base year emissions recalculations;
	\item For each S3 category, any biogenic C0\textsubscript{2} emissions reported separately; 
	\item For each S3 category, a description of the various types and sources of data, including activity data, emission factors and global warming potential (GWP) values, used to calculate emissions, and a description of the data quality of reported emissions data;
	\item For each S3 category, a description of the methodologies alongside allocation methods and assumptions used to calculate S3 emissions;
	\item For each S3 category, the percentage of emissions calculated using the data obtained from suppliers or other value chain partners.
\end{itemize}
These reporting requirements are clearly not trivial. Indeed, such is their nature and extent that some have concluded that they are practically unworkable in their current form \cite{patchell2018}. The next Section explores the common problems that follow from these standards.

\section{Data coverage and quality}\label{sec:Scope3Challenges}

Early studies using nascent S3 assessments found data that were largely incomplete and lacking rigour \cite{DownieStubbs2013}. Unfortunately, even after a decade, a report by FTSE-Russell concluded that the data are still characterised by large gaps and poor quality \cite{ftserussell2024}. This suggests that something in the data generating process (as outlined above in Section \ref{sec:Scope3Reporting}) could be driving these inadequate outcomes. This Section describes the main shortcomings of S3 data. Beginning with a review of challenges inherent in the data generating process, I subsequently highlight the problems this causes for analysis. 

\subsection{Issues with the data generating process}\label{sec:DataGenIssues}

Klaaßen and Stoll \cite{klassenstoll2021} identify three causes of error in firms' S3 GHG emissions data: reporting inconsistency, boundary incompleteness and activity exclusion. 

\subsubsection{Reporting inconsistency}

Firms may disclose their S3 GHG emissions through corporate channels including annual reports and corporate social responsibility reports (CSRs), to independent third parties such as the CDP (formerly the Carbon Disclosure Project), or to their regulatory agencies. However, there is no need for these disclosures to be consistent with each other as they are written for different purposes and audiences. In line with the expectations of stakeholder theory, studies have found that on average, GHG emissions disclosed in corporate reports are lower than those reported to the CDP \cite{depoers2016, klassenstoll2021}. As corporate reports are less prescriptive than the CDP questionnaire, it appears that firms use them to paint a better picture of their GHG emissions to the wider public by suppressing their bad outcomes and championing positive measures in so-called ``green-washing''. Notwithstanding this, different figures between reports do not necessarily imply fraudulent or dishonest disclosure: it may simply reflect the choice of valid but different methodologies \cite{depoers2016}. In any case, the upshot is that even for a given firm in a given year which self-reports, there is potentially no consistent source of ground truth. 

\subsubsection{Boundary incompleteness}

For each of the 15 S3 categories, the Protocol recommends a minimum boundary of what to include, and firms are then encouraged to choose the most appropriate calculation method depending on data quality and quantity. Calculation methods can be top-down (input-output based), bottom-up (process-based), or a hybrid of the two which begins bottom-up and then fills in the gaps top-down. For the most material categories, firms are expected to use primary data, i.e. data sourced directly from other firms. However, there is often limited data transparency across the value chain and so this option is commonly unavailable. Where secondary data such as industry reports and production volumes is also missing, boundary incompleteness follows. Some scholars such as Patchell \cite{patchell2018} argue that this is an intractable problem due to the complexity of global supply chains and the prohibitive costs involved.\footnote{This might explain why despite the US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) previously intending to introduce compulsory S3 disclosures, a move that some argued was a vital step for improving coverage \cite{lgim2023}, it has instead decided against this course of action following objections raised in industry consultations \cite{sec2024}.}

\subsubsection{Activity exclusion}\label{sec:ActivityExclusion}

Finally, even when firms report their S3 emissions, they may choose to omit certain relevant categories entirely. Despite the encouragements of standard setters to report S3 emissions that are considered `material' (ISSB) \cite{ifrs2023}, `appropriate' (TCFD) \cite{tcfd2021}, or `significant' (GRI) \cite{gri2016},  firms often report what is easier to measure (e.g. business travel) rather than what is important to their carbon footprint. For example, in their 2022 report, CDP found that only 36\% of reporting firms disclosed their Purchased Goods and Services (Category 1) emissions, which are highly relevant for almost every sector \cite{cdp2022, ftserussell2024}. As this remains a persistent problem, FTSE Russell \cite{ftserussell2024} has recommended that companies begin by reporting the two most material categories for their sector, which on average should encompass over 80\% of their total S3 emissions. However, as will be shown Figure \ref{fig:Scope3Coverage} and discussed further in Section \ref{sec:Coverage}, only around half of S3 disclosures satisfy the materiality requirement.

\subsection{Consequential problems for data analysis}
\subsubsection{Missing data}
The main reason for the paucity of data comes from the nature of S3 emissions: in contrast to S1 and S2, they are by definition emissions in the value chain beyond the firm's direct control or ownership, and therefore the data are often outside a firm's information set. Ideally, firms would be able to share data across the value chain, however there are significant barriers to doing this, including legal and regulatory challenges, interoperability and data privacy \cite{StenzelWaichman2023}. 
\\\\
Accordingly, across various datasets, S3 coverage typically exists for only around half of firms.\footnote{45\% of FTSE All-World constituents report S3 data as of 2021 \cite{ftserussell2024}. For firms reporting to the CDP, 41\%  disclose emissions for one or more S3 category in 2022 \cite{cdp2023}. Bloomberg and Refinitiv Eikon appear to have slightly higher proportions \cite{Nguyenetal2023}.} Furthermore, as reporting remains voluntary and the propensity to report depends on firms' characteristics such as size, sector, and reporting jurisdiction, the missing data should be assumed to be `missing not at random' (MNAR). However, current estimation models used by data providers often do not take this uncertainty into account, leading to misleading prediction intervals and inaccurate predictions \cite{olesiewicz2021}. In this way, missing data is closely related to biased data, the second problem in analysis. 

\subsubsection{Biased data}
Whereas a firm which is non-reporting is conspicuous by its absence, a more subtle problem is when a firm under-reports due to boundary incompleteness or activity exclusion. Without scrutiny of a firm's methodology, the researcher might assume that the reported total S3 emissions is an accurate reflection of the firm's carbon footprint, rather than a reflection of what they choose to measure and report. This problem also further degrades the data generating process; as many firms under-report their emissions, firms which estimate their emissions using peer averages also under-report as a result \cite{Hoepner2021}. Both problems of missing data (non-reporting) and biased data (under-reporting) are illustrated by Figure \ref{fig:Scope3Coverage}, which shows that most firms don't report their S3 data (i.e. one minus the orange line), and for those that do, they mostly report immaterial figures (i.e. the gap between orange and green line). 

\subsubsection{Volatile data}
The relative immaturity of S3 reporting is reflected in the high volatility of the data (see Figure \ref{fig:Scope3Volatility}). As of 2021, over half of the disclosed values vary by over 20\% year-on-year, and over a third vary by at least 50\%. Although this is something that could improve over time as reporting practices evolve, the evidence suggests that the issue is still getting worse \cite{ftserussell2024}. Moreover, even when FTSE-Russell considered S1 and S2 data which is much less volatile than S3, they found that irrespective of the methodology used the predictive power of estimates was low and imprecise.\footnote{See Figure \ref{fig:FTSERussellResults} and in Section \ref{sec:ModelComparison}.} Therefore, it is probable that any t-step ahead forecasting exercise using past S3 data will be characterised by statistical insignificance and wide confidence intervals derived from noisy data.

\subsubsection{Lack of consistency and comparability}
In addition to the issues already discussed, there are three reasons why S3 data may not be comparable across firms. First, despite the specifics covered in Section \ref{sec:Scope3Reporting}, the GHG Protocol provides firms with a wide room for manoeuvre regarding which accounting and reporting methodologies they use. For example, firms must make a number of choices including their organisational boundaries, which categories to report, the calculation approach for each category, data sources, how to allocate emissions, whether to report optional extras above the minimum boundaries and so on. As long as these choices can be justified according to the five key accounting principles (which are relevance, completeness, consistency, transparency, accuracy) then they are said to conform to S3 reporting standards \cite{ghgscope32013}. This methodological latitude opens the possibility for meaningful heterogeneity in firms' S3 accounting and reporting: accordingly, while firms may be consistent with respect to themselves, and with respect to the GHG Protocol itself, aggregating firms together in a portfolio risks comparing apples and oranges. 
\\ \\
Second, the GHG Protocol and S3 Guidance is often complimented with standard setters' frameworks and legal rules. While these may intend to be aligned to the GHG Protocol, they invariably introduce particular interpretations and requirements which their signatories are expected to adopt. This could lead to inconsistencies, especially between sectors as they may follow their own industry-specific practices. For example, many financial institutions have adopted the recommendations of the Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials (PCAF) in an attempt to improve the reporting quality for Category 15 of S3 emissions \cite{PCAF2022}. However, as pointed out by Tang et al. \cite{Tang2023}, the practical considerations of GHG estimation means that PCAF's framework does not align with the reality of availability, reliability and confidence of the ``highest quality'' corporate reported emissions data. This leads to increased non-standardisation, more inaccuracy and further concerns of inconsistency. 
\\ \\
Third, when investors wish to compare the GHG emissions of many firms, perhaps to evaluate the transition risk of their portfolio, they frequently turn to data providers such as Bloomberg, CDP, ISS, MSCI, Sustainalytics, Thomson Reuters, and Trucost. However, due to missing data, these providers often use estimates to fill in the gaps. Unfortunately, each use their own methodologies with varying transparency. These different approaches can compound the inconsistency across the data and make it challenging for people to know what the ``real'' S3 emissions are for a collection of firms. Busch et al. \cite{Busch2022} and Kalesnik et al. \cite{KalesnikVitali2022}  found that third party estimated data was less consistent than data stemming directly from corporate reports, and so the choice of data provider might significantly affect empirical results.\footnote{Busch et al. also found that this third party estimated data inconsistency was increasing over time, although this has been challenged with more recent data \cite{Swinkels2024}.} Moreover, third party inconsistencies extend beyond differences in estimation methodologies. Even for values reported by firms, surprisingly Nguyen et al. \cite{Nguyenetal2023} found only 68\% of data points being identical between Bloomberg and Refinitiv Eikon. 

\section{Estimating S3 emissions}\label{sec:Scope3Modelling}

This Section reviews the academic and data providers' estimation strategies for S3 emissions. Unfortunately, there are not many quantitative papers on S3 emissions in a macro context, such as for a country or industry, due to the reasons mentioned in the previous Section \cite{Hettler2024}.  
\\ \\
As there is no industry consensus on the best approach for estimating GHG emissions \cite{FTSERussell2022}, the literature offers several approaches, which Nguyen et al. \cite{nguyen2021} categorise as naive, empirical and ML. I employ this same framework to explore S3 estimation. 

\subsection{Naive estimation}
First, there are naive approaches which use simple averages. They typically involve several sub-models which are then aggregated based on data availability. Common strategies include extrapolation from past emissions, using the firm's consumption or production figures, or simple sector/industry averages. While third party data providers often use more sophisticated models for S1 and S2, naive approaches are typically used for S3 estimation because they are easy to calculate, simple to explain, and sidestep some of the challenging data quality issues. Companies using these approaches include the ISS \cite{iss2023} and the London Stock Exchange Group (LSEG) \cite{lseg2023}.

\subsection{Empirical estimation}

Second, empirical estimation comprises regression methods which model the linear relationships between variables. While this is a common approach in the literature for estimating S1 and S2 emissions, researchers often don't extend this approach to S3 due to data quality concerns \cite{GoldhammerEtAl2017, KalesnikVitali2022}. Nevertheless, a few attempts have been made.
\\ \\
The CDP uses a gamma generalised linear model using both revenue and activity information \cite{cdp2023b}. They choose the gamma distribution to accommodate the positive but heteroskedastic nature of the data. The target variables are average activity-group revenue intensities reported to the CDP, with revenue broken down into 44 variables. To prevent overfitting, model selection is optimised by pruning on activity groupings which are chosen to maximise the Akaike Information Criterion for each of the 15 S3 categories. Given the hundreds of possible variables that may be used, others have tackled model specification by using regularisation to automate feature inclusion \cite{CaiXia2023}.
\\ \\
A key drawback for these more traditional statistical approaches is that estimates rely on parametric assumptions that, if not met, will fail to provide the best, linear, and unbiased estimators. Especially important in the context of S3, Olesiewicz et al. \cite{olesiewicz2021} show that if the data is not missing at random, then the regression estimates will be biased.

\subsection{Machine learning techniques}
Thirdly, Nguyen et al. \cite{Nguyenetal2023} explore the performance of ML models for predicting S3 emissions against naive and statistical approaches.\footnote{At the time of writing this is the only previous ML paper on S3 estimation, and so this forms the main source of comparison with my own work. For the sake of completeness, there is also a paper by Serafeim and Velez Caicedo \cite{Serafeim2022}. However, it remains a working paper only, has no citations, and reports extremely high predictive accuracy.} The two baseline models are an industry-fill model and a naive OLS model. The industry-fill model simply uses the median of disclosed emissions data of a firm's nearest available peer group. The naive OLS model predicts S3 emissions at a categorical level using just revenue, total employees, and a set of dummy industry indicators. 
\\\\
For linear models, given the limited number of S3 observations, using an extended set of predictors may lead to multicollinearity issues \cite{nguyen2021}. To avoid this, the authors use the forward-backward stepwise regression to automatically include the relevant predictors and exclude irrelevant ones. They further employ an elastic net to also address multicollinearity. 
\\\\
For their ML models, they use tree-based ensemble models due to the success of these kinds of models elsewhere in the emissions modelling literature \cite{nguyen2021, han2021}. This success is in part because these models capture non-linearities and correlations well, whilst also improving the stability and interpretability of the coefficients. Specifically, the authors employ both a Random Forest and Extreme Gradient Boosting. Finally, they also use a linear tree/forest approach which they hypothesise would lead to better predictive results because it combines the learning ability of decision trees with the extrapolation power of linear models. 
\\\\
The authors report big accuracy gains from predicting each category separately and then aggregating them up to estimate total S3 emissions for each firm. Upstream categories appear easier to predict than downstream categories. Surprisingly, out of all ML models, only the linear forest consistently outperforms the baseline models in predicting total S3 emissions using both aggregated data and category-level data. Furthermore, when considering the prediction accuracy of absolute emission values, even the best prediction model has substantial error. Specifically, the stepwise OLS generates a median absolute error of 72.3\% on aggregated S3 emissions; this means that only 30\% of the predicted emissions values are within +/- 50\% of the actual reported emissions. Thus, the ML approaches tried so far appear disappointing both relative to simpler models and in absolute terms for predictive accuracy.
\\\\
The Nguyen et al. paper \cite{Nguyenetal2023}, being the only past paper using ML techniques to estimate S3 emissions, will be the main point of comparison with respect to my own results in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Results}.

\section{Features and target variables}\label{sec:FeaturesAndTargetVariables}

In line with previous empirical papers, I use firm fundamentals and characteristics to model and predict emissions. These features reflect aspects of a firm's size and nature of business, and these, in turn, relate to its carbon footprint. 
\\\\
There is often a tension between the availability of data and relevance to the model. For example, some have used the number of employees as a feature \cite{nguyen2021}; it is a useful proxy for a firm's size and it directly relates to some S3 categories such as employee commuting. However, it is not a standard metric in annual accounting and reporting standards, so the data is often unchanging for many years or missing altogether. Given the paucity of emissions data, I do not include features with over $10\%$ of their values missing. This means that I do not include features such as: gross profit, current assets, current liabilities, inventories and cost of revenue, even though they may be theoretically relevant.

\subsection{Feature descriptions}\label{sec:FeatureDescriptions}
\subsubsection{Feature construction}\label{sec:FeatureConstruction}
Table \ref{tab:features} lists the raw features I query from LSEG. This forms the basis for my sample construction. In a few cases, I perform simple aggregations to construct new features. First, I map the country of risk (or HQ country code if the former is missing) into either ``Advanced'' or ``Emerging'' buckets as set out by the International Monetary Fund (IMF) World Economic Outlook database for 2023 \cite{IMFWEO2023}. This is an important feature to include as, first, advanced economies have higher reporting standards and so higher rates of GHG disclosure, and second firms in advanced economies are likely to be less carbon intensive as their energy mix likely includes a higher renewable share and more modern technology. Some past studies have tried to use a firm's energy use, map that to their country's energy mix and include that in their feature set. However, energy use data is often missing and country energy mix data is infrequent. Therefore I have chosen to keep my model parsimonious by just using the country risk classifications of either ``Advanced'' or ``Emerging'', perhaps risking oversimplification and risking my models' prediction ability.
\\\\
Second, I construct emission aggregates. I sum S1 and S2 emissions to capture a firm's GHG footprint outside of S3. I also combine the eight S3 upstream categories into a single S3 upstream value, and also do likewise for the seven S3 downstream categories. While Nguyen et al.'s study model each category, I have fewer data points in my sample \cite{Nguyenetal2023}. In addition, category disclosure rates vary significantly with some disclosure rates very low (see Figure \ref{fig:S3DisclosureByCategory}). Therefore, while it would be ideal to model each category separately and aggregate up into S3 upstream and downstream, this is not a viable approach in my case.\footnote{Nevertheless, in Section \ref{sec:CategoryPrediction} I discuss category-level modelling further and attempt to model category one.} Therefore, I simply sum across the relevant categories. In turn, I sum the S3 upstream and S3 downstream totals into a single S3 category total. 
\\\\
Third, I calculate emission intensities by dividing the emission features by revenue. This means that an emission value, reported in metric tonnes of CO2 equivalent, can be transformed into an intensity, measured in metric tonnes of CO2 equivalent per million of USD revenue. In a world with full data availability, I would like to model the emissions profile of each component of a firm's activity. Lacking this, company emission intensity allows me to compare across firms more easily by normalising with size/activity.  

\subsubsection{Firm characteristics}
A firm's carbon footprint is most likely to be tied to its scale of operations, therefore I use several measures of this. First, I use firm revenue, as carbon emissions are a by-product of revenue generation and this has been included in all prior models in the empirical literature. Second, I use the market capitalisation in the month closest to the firm's fiscal (i.e. reporting) period; market capitalisation can be thought of as an overall measure of the company's worth through investors' assessments of a firm's assets, liabilities and future cash flows. Third, the scale of operations in a firm may be indicated by its asset size. Therefore, I use total assets, intangibles, and net property, plant and equipment values. The latter is especially useful as high values may indicate the presence of physical plants and equipment (PPE) that are likely to be more closely related to GHGs. On the other hand, firms with high intangible assets are less likely to have a substantial carbon footprint.
\\\\
Next, I include both earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) and earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization (EBITDA). These can be useful proxies for business activity. For example, some firms with very stable cash flows are utility firms which may have a substantial carbon footprint. Meanwhile, other firms like commodity producers may exhibit highly volatile earnings depending on changes in spot prices. Having both EBIT and EBITDA also allows me to consider the impact of asset depreciation; higher depreciation might mean firms invest in more equipment, thereby modernising, and so reducing their carbon footprint. I also include other earnings metrics such as net cash flow and receivables. 
\\\\
In addition, I include a firm's long term debt, as I suspect that higher long term debt restricts a firm's ability to borrow, invest, and modernise, which may raise their carbon footprint. Most importantly for the business model, I include a dummy for the firm's peer group, which is an indicator of a firm's economic sector, industry, or activity depending on the appropriate level of granularity. This is described in more detail in Section \ref{sec:PeerGroups}.
\\\\
Capital expenditures (capex) is another important variable I include. Adoption of modern technologies and equipment can be indicated by high levels of capex. As such, it is a feature that goes some way to explain a firm's level of technology which has clear implications for its carbon footprint via efficiencies. 

\subsubsection{Environmental and regulatory context}
The final features I include concern the firm's environmental and regulatory context. I begin with each firm's so-called primary country of risk. According to Refinitiv, this uses the StarMine Countries of Risk Model which is based on four sources of data: its revenue distribution by geography, the location of a company's headquarters, the country where its primary equity security listing trades, and its financial reporting currency. Together, this gives a rounded view of which country the company is most exposed to.\footnote{This is a better measure than the HQ location which may be skewed by tax regimes.} I then bucket this variable into ``advanced'' or ``emerging'' economy dummies as aforementioned. 
\\\\
I also include measures relating to the firm's policies on emissions reduction which situates the firm's efforts within its sector. The Target Emissions Score measures to what extent firms have set targets on  emissions reduction. The Policy Emissions Score measures how the firm uses its policies to improve emissions reductions; this includes measures relating to the mechanisms or programmes in place, using a firm's formal, documented processes for controlling emissions and driving improvement. Both of these emission scores range from 0 to 100 and follow a transparent, detailed, fully-automated methodology \cite{RefinitivESG2023}.
\\\\
As well as the features outlined above, there are production-specific features that I considered but, on reflection, decided to exclude. These include measures such as how much crude oil or natural gas is produced. Such measures only apply to a very small number of firms in the sample, and while this would provide more colour on these firms, it clearly would not extend to building a general model that spans across sectors.

\subsection{Target variable}
I model three target variables for S3 emissions: total, upstream and downstream (refer to Section \ref{sec:FeatureConstruction}). Lastly, my final target variable is total S1 and S2 emissions. This allows me to compare against findings from earlier studies which only model emissions for S1 and S2. It also provides me a way to see if there are any major differences between S1 and S2, and S3 modelling. While S1 and S2 emissions are conceptually distinct from S3, a simple plot of total S1 and S2 vs total S3 emissions reveals, surprisingly, a significant, positive relationship (see Figure \ref{fig:S123DotPlot} in Appendix \ref{sec:chartbook}). Following this relationship, I found that including S1 and S2 emissions improves predictive accuracy for S3 emissions. Therefore, for all S3 target variables, S1+S2 emissions are also included as a feature.   

\section{Sample construction}\label{sec:SampleConstruction}

Ahead of modelling GHG emissions, I do some work to prepare my data. First, I call the data from the Refinitiv database using the API wrapped in the Python package \textsc{refinitiv}. To be assured of the results, I also separately call the data via the Excel plug-in and perform a systematic comparison to ensure they return identical results. 
\\\\
I begin by retrieving the static data on all FTSE All-World index constituents as of 2022-12-31, such as primary country of risk and sector information. I then merge on a set of fundamentals data. Finally, I add on a snapshot of each firm's market capitalisation using the nearest available (backward-looking) data point relative to the fundamental data. 
\\\\
With the raw data loaded, I undertake a series of filtering processes, visualised in the Sankey diagram in Figure \ref{fig:Sankey}. There are three important aspects of this process.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{CarbonForecaster/report/figures/preprocessing_sankey.png}
    \caption{Sankey diagram of sample construction}
	\label{fig:Sankey}

First, most of the filtering occurs by removing observations without S1 and S2 data. This shows that even with the better covered scopes, coverage isn't guaranteed as reporting stringency varies according to region. Second, I have chosen to remove all financial firms as their fundamentals are often not an accurate reflection of their underlying business, and it is common practice to consider only non-financial firms in cross-sector analysis. Third, as a final stage of filtering I remove observations with abnormal revenue or CO2e values based on year-over-year ratios. Specifically, I identify instruments with extreme changes in revenue, S1 and S2 CO2e, S3 upstream CO2e, and S3 downstream CO2e by calculating the ratio of maximum to minimum values over time for each instrument. Then, observations with ratios exceeding the 99th percentile for any of these metrics are considered abnormal and removed from consideration. 
\\\\
In addition, I took steps to improve data coverage and numerical stability of some of the features. Fundamentals data were forward and backward filled to account for missing values. In addition, net cash flows were winsorized given the high skew and kurtosis exhibited.

\section{Sample stratification into peer groups}\label{sec:PeerGroups}

Firms belong to different sectors which influences their GHG emissions as discussed in Section \ref{sec:SampleConstruction}. Effective stratification ensures that both the training and test sets maintain similar proportions of firms from each sector. Without this stratification, a random split could result in an under- or over-representation of certain sectors in the test set, potentially compromising model performance. By stratifying the data to mirror the real-world distribution, I can enhance the model's ability to generalise, especially when sector classes are imbalanced (see Figure \ref{fig:EconSectorBreakdownS32022}).\\

\chapter{Modelling approach}\label{Chapter:ModellingApproach}

Chapters \ref{Chapter:Background} and \ref{Chapter:Data} have set out the policy background, previous empirical efforts, and some high-level data exploration. This Chapter introduces the four key dimensions that will determine the specifics of my modelling approach. As a simple framework to orient the discussion, consider the regression problem in abstract in Equation \ref{eq:AbstractRegressionProblem}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{y}_{it} = f\left(\mathbf{X}_{it}\right)
    \label{eq:AbstractRegressionProblem}
\end{equation}
My goal is to predict S3 emissions  using an appropriate \textbf{target variable} ($\mathbf{y}$), a \textbf{set of explanatory features} ($\mathbf{X}$), over a \textbf{set of observations} ($i, t$), using the best \textbf{model} $f$. I consider each of these dimensions in turn.

\section{The target variable}\label{sec:TargetVariable}

There are four targets to predict: S3 upstream emissions, S3 downstream emissions, total S3 category emissions, and (for comparison), total S1 and S2 emissions. There are two ways to measure these targets. First, I could use absolute emission levels. Second, I could use emission intensity (absolute emissions divided by revenue). 
\\\\
As a first step in my empirical approach, I will try modelling with absolute levels versus intensities and compare the results in predictive ability. 

\section{Rationalising the feature choice set}\label{sec:ChoosingFeatureSet}
The second important choice is which set of explanatory features to use. The choice of features is driven by a combination of both data availability and \textit{a priori} theory. One potential issue is the presence of multicollinearity i.e. where several features are highly correlated with each other. Indeed, the correlation matrix presented in Figure \ref{fig:FeatureCorrleationMatrix} reveals that some variables are highly correlated, e.g. EBIT and EBITDA. This has the potential to cause computational problems and makes precise estimation of coefficients more difficult. 

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{CarbonForecaster/report/figures/feature_correlation_matrix.png}
    \caption{Correlation matrix between numeric features}
	\label{fig:FeatureCorrleationMatrix}
\end{figure}

An attempt to deal with this issue from Nguyen et al's previous study uses a Forward-Backward Stepwise Regression, which automatically includes relevant predictors (e.g. $<1\%$ significant level) into the model and excludes irrelevant ones (e.g. $>5\%$ significant level) \cite{Nguyenetal2023}. When I take this algorithmic approach to feature selection I retain only 15 features, with the vast majority of dummy variables removed. While on the surface this may appear to be an attractive solution, as pointed out by Harrell (2015) this ``violates every principle of statistical estimation and hypothesis testing'' \cite{Harrell2015}.
\\\\
Another common tactic to select relevant features is to use feature attribution (FA) methods. These methods are popular in explainable AI to help people understand how the inputs of a ML model contribute to its output. Common methods include perturbation-based ones such as \texttt{LIME}~\cite{ribeiro2016why} and \texttt{SHAP}~\cite{lundberg2017unified}, or gradient-based ones such as Integrated Gradients~\cite{sundararajan2017axiomatic} and \texttt{DeepLIFT}~\cite{shrikumar2019learning}. These approaches reduce the complexity of a model's mathematical logic into a set of numerical importance scores for each feature. 
\\\\
Unfortunately, these FA methods cannot be straightforwardly applied to time series data. This is because there are temporal dependencies in time series data meaning that data points are dependent on previous values. By contrast, methods such as LIME or SHAP are designed for independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. By permuting or masking certain features, they break this dependency and produce results that are unrepresentative of true behaviour. 
\\
Moreover, by evaluating individual features, these FA methods often overlook how the sequence of past observations contributes to the current value, in effect ignoring the data's autocorrelation. Furthermore, in a time series context, feature importance may vary over time, so the feature attribution becomes dynamic rather than static.  
\\\\
Given that stepwise regression and standard FA methods are not statistically valid approaches to feature selection, I opt for a different way. From the set of all possible features described in Section \ref{sec:FeatureDescriptions} with sufficient coverage, I create three sets: the extended set, the core set, and the limited set, as described by Table \ref{tab:FeatureBuckets}.

The extended set of features includes the whole range of metrics in Section \ref{sec:FeatureDescriptions}. The core set includes six key measures, and the limited set only three. All sets include S1 and S2 emissions as well as a group of dummy variables. Similar to selecting the target variable, for selecting which features to use I try all different combinations to see which sets, in general, produce results with higher predictive accuracy.
\\\\
As well as feature choice, feature scaling is important in many ML algorithms. This is because they often use distance-based measures (e.g. K-Nearest Neighbours), gradient-based optimisations (e.g. neural networks) or penalisation methods (e.g. elastic nets); without scaling these models may fail to find the optimal solution or may take a very long time to converge.
\\\\
Log-scaling is often used in financial settings due to the skewed nature of the data; this maps orders of magnitude differences into a linear scale. I use log-scaling for the core feature set and the limited feature set. For the extended feature set, I instead use robust scaling (value minus the median, divided by the interquartile range) as some of the cash flow variables such as EBIT may be negative, and the log function is not defined for that domain. 


\section{Observation subsets}\label{sec:ChoosingObsSet}

In normal circumstances, I would use all data to train or test my models. However, one worry is that the impact of the Covid-19 pandemic may feed anomalous and unrepresentative observations into the model training, reducing predictive ability. Another major concern about S3 data is that firms may only partially report, leaving open the potential that they provide S3 figures that do not accurately capture their true carbon footprint.
\\\\
To mitigate these concerns, I use three different observation sets. My first set simply includes all observations over the date range (2016 to 2022). My second set (``ExCovid'') excludes all observations from the fiscal year 2021. This is an admittedly crude approach because different firms and industries were impacted by Covid at different times. However, lacking the data to incorporate this more precisely, and given the non-standardised reporting dates of firms' disclosures, this provides me an acceptable approximation. 
\\\\
Finally, to tackle the problem of under-reporting, LSEG has proposed ``material'' S3 categories for firms in each sector \cite{ftserussell2024}.\footnote{See also \cite{CDPTechnicalNote2023} for a technical note on the relevance of S3 categories by sector.} This picks out the two most important S3 categories for each firm peer group. For example, they report that for technology firms, 88 percent of their S3 intensity is composed of their two material categories. Therefore, as a third set of observations to experiment with, I use the subset that contain non-missing values in material categories for the firm's peer group. This also has the desirable property of trying to predict material emissions, and not merely what firms report as their emissions. Hence, such predictions could be used as a basis to guard against under-reporting in line with a precautionary principle (see Section \ref{sec:PrecuationaryPrinciple} for a more in-depth discussion on this point). 
\\\\
To calculate which sectors are material, I follow a similar approach to LSEG in their report \cite{ftserussell2024}. First, I calculate the median S3 intensity for each S3 category in each TRBC business sector. Next, I rank these medians and designate the two largest the ``material'' S3 categories for that business sector. Finally, for each firm, I flag that they are material reporters if they report the two highest S3 categories for their business sector. To mitigate against underestimating material categories with low reporting rates (see Figure \ref{fig:S3DisclosureByCategory}), this is performed on all available reporting companies from each category. For most business sectors, the material categories are purchased goods and services, and use of sold products (that is, S3 categories 1 and 11 respectively. See \ref{tab:MaterialCatsPerBusinessSector} for a full breakdown.)

Having covered the general modelling process, it remains to  introduce the eight model types I employ to predict GHG emissions. In keeping with the labels of ``naive'', ``statistical'' and ``machine learning'' used from earlier literature (see Section \ref{sec:Scope3Modelling}), I use two naive models, two statistical models, and four ML models. In addition, for regression problems such as this, many techniques have been developed by the ML community precisely because there is not a one-method-fits-all paradigm. Furthermore, estimating GHG emissions is still a nascent field, and I believe it is reasonable to try many different approaches to see what performs better.

Moreover, it is also important to consider, briefly, the model types I do not use, and how this relates to my overall approach to time series forecasting in this context. There are two common approaches to time series forecasting. First, there is  ``direct forecasting'' which involves predicting future values of a time series by using past observations. In other words, the goal is to estimate the value of the target variable at time $t+i$ based on the data available up to time $t$. Second, there is forecasting with covariates whereby a model learns the relationship between the target variable and one or more covariates during a training phase. In the test phase, the model predicts the value of the target variable based on the values of the covariates. This is also sometimes termed ``multivariate time series forecasting''. 
\\\\
It is clear from Section \ref{sec:Coverage} that, because coverage has greatly improved in later years, the approach of direct forecasting would risk reducing the training dataset too much because I would lose a lot of observations by even excluding just the year 2022. Due to the nature of the data available, therefore, I have adopted the second approach of forecasting with covariates. In order to use methods that respect the characteristics of my data, I have also excluded certain models from consideration.
\\\\
Firstly, I have avoided autoregressive integrated moving average (ARIMA) and vector autoregression (VAR) models which are common time series forecasting approaches in other domains. This is because these models are better at handling small, less noisy datasets. Additionally, they were developed to model univariate processes or only a handful of variables. Secondly, I have avoided using transformer-based deep learning models, such as the temporal fusion transformer (TFT). This is because these models are typically better at capturing more intricate time dependencies such as trend, seasonality and ad-hoc components such as holiday periods. By contrast, my emissions data is annual, limited, and too volatile to exhibit any clear trend. In addition, the more established deep learning approaches such as RNNs and LSTMs were designed with direct forecasting in mind which also makes them unsuitable for application in this context. 

\subsection{Naive models}

I use two naive models - the industry fill and OLS regression - which provide simple baselines against which to benchmark the more sophisticated models to follow. 

\subsubsection{Industry fill}

Beginning with the most simple, the industry-fill model (IFM) estimates the carbon emissions of a firm by using the median among its peer group in the training data.\footnote{The name ``industry-fill'' is found elsewhere in the literature because it is typically the firm's industry from which the median is derived. In my context, I also use the firm's nearest available peer group as described in Section \ref{sec:PeerGroups}. However, to remain consistent with the literature I use the term ``industry-fill'' rather than ``peer-fill''.} This provides a baseline against which to assess more sophisticated techniques, and it is a typical benchmark used in other studies such as in Klaaßen and Stoll \cite{klassenstoll2021} and Nguyen et al \cite{nguyen2021, Nguyenetal2023}. Just as importantly, it is also a standard method used by the major commercial data providers such as LSEG \cite{ftserussell2024} and Bloomberg \cite{Bloomberg2023}, at least as a starting point.
\\\\
The IFM for total metric tonnes of CO2 equivalent for firm $i$ in peer group $IND$ at time $t$ is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathit{Emission_{i\in{IND},t}}= \mathit{median}(\mathit{Emission_{IND})}
\end{equation}

The IFM has several advantages: it is tractable, easy to compute, the data is widely available, it is not skewed by outliers, and it aligns with the `precautionary principle' emphasised in the literature \cite{Hoepner2021, olesiewicz2023}, such that firms which don't report are assumed to be at least as emitting as their peers who do report. 
\\\\
However, there are plainly limitations to the IFM. First, it ignores all firm-specific information which may be relevant, such as firm size. Second, even within peer groups there is still a wide range of GHG emission values. Notwithstanding these drawbacks, it is a useful starting point.

\subsubsection{OLS Regression}

Ordinary least-squares (OLS) regression is a technique that estimates the conditional mean of the dependent variable given some independent variables. Put another way, it models the average relationship between the predictors and an outcome using a single line. This line represents the best fit for all data points based on the mean of the dependent variable. OLS is one way to derive the line of best fit.
\\\\
Another common baseline is OLS regression based on a proxy for firm size and some fixed effects. For example, Kalesnik et al. \cite{KalesnikVitali2022} regress log emissions on log of net sales along with industry, year and country fixed effects, finding that this explains over 80 percent of in-sample variation. 
\\\\
Formally, let  $y$ denote the dependent variable represented as an $n \times 1$ vector of observed values, $X$ an $n \times m$ matrix of explanatory variables, and $\beta$ an $m \times 1$ vector of coefficients to be estimated. OLS fits a parameter vector $\beta$ such that the predicted values $\hat{y}=X\beta$ are as close as possible to the observed values in $y$. This is done by minimising the sum of the squared residuals (i.e. differences) between the observed values $y$ and the predicted values $\hat{y}$. Accordingly, the goal of OLS is to minimise the objective (loss) function:
\begin{align}
    \text{minimise } \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 = \text{minimise } (y - X\beta)^\top (y - X\beta),
\end{align}
\\\\
To find the vector $\beta$ that minimises the sum of squared residuals, one takes the derivative of the objective function with respect to $\beta$ and sets it to zero: 
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \left( (y - X\beta)^\top (y - X\beta) \right) = -2X^\top(y - X\beta) = 0.
\end{align}
\\\\
Solving for $\beta$, one gets: 
\begin{align}
    X^\top X \beta = X^\top y \Leftrightarrow  \beta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y
\end{align}
Assuming that the matrix $X$ is of full-rank and therefore invertible.\footnote{In practice, inverting the $X$ matrix can be computationally expensive, so \textsc{Scikit-Learn} does the optimisation algorithmically instead.}
\\\\
As it is intended to provide a second, simple baseline, I use the following model for estimating S3 emissions by OLS:
\begin{align}
\mathit{logEmission_{i,t}} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \mathit{logRV_{i,t}} + \beta_2 \mathit{logMCAP_{i,t}} + \beta_3 \mathit{log(S1+S2)_{i,t}} \nonumber \\
&\quad + \sum_j \gamma_j \mathit{IND_j} + \sum_t \delta_t \mathit{YEAR_t} + \theta \mathit{CC} + \epsilon_{i,t}
\end{align}

In this equation, \( \mathit{logEmission_{i,t}} \) represents the log of S3 emissions for firm \( i \) in year \( t \), which is the dependent variable. \( \beta_0 \) is the intercept, representing the baseline level of \( \mathit{logEmission_{i,t}} \) when all other variables are zero. \( \mathit{logRV_{i,t}} \) is the log of the revenue for firm \( i \) in year \( t \), and \( \beta_1 \) is its associated coefficient, capturing the effect of revenue on emissions. \( \mathit{logMCAP_{i,t}} \) is the log of the market capitalisation of firm \( i \) in year \( t \), with \( \beta_2 \) representing the effect of firm size on S3 emissions. \( \mathit{log(S1+S2)_{i,t}} \) represents the log of combined S1 and S2 emissions, and \( \beta_3 \) captures the contribution of direct and energy-related emissions to the prediction of S3 emissions. \( \sum_j \gamma_j \mathit{IND_j} \) includes a set of industry dummy variables (\( \mathit{IND_j} \)) for firm \( i \), where \( \gamma_j \) are the coefficients that control for industry-specific effects. \( \sum_t \delta_t \mathit{YEAR_t} \) represents year fixed effects, with \( \delta_t \) as the coefficients controlling for temporal variations. \( \mathit{CC} \) is a dummy variable that indicates whether a firm belongs to an advanced or emerging economy, with \( \theta \) as its coefficient, capturing the effect of country classification on emissions. \( \epsilon_{i,t} \) is the error term, accounting for the variability in emissions that is not explained by the model's predictors.
\\\\
OLS will act as a second baseline. However, given the objective function is based on the $\ell_2$ norm it is likely to be undermined by the presence of outliers. This leads me to consider the statistical models which employ regularisations.  

\subsection{Statistical models}

\subsubsection{Quantile Regression}

Whereas OLS regression estimates the conditional mean of the dependent variable given the independent variables, quantile regression (QR) estimates the conditional quantiles of the dependent variable given the independent variables. Rather than focusing on just the central mean tendency, QR allows one to model the distribution of the independent variable. This is useful in the case when the data distribution is highly skewed by outliers and so the mean can be distorted by extreme values. It is also helpful in cases where the effect of predictors is not uniform in the distribution.\footnote{Also known as ``heteroscedasticity'', where the variance of residuals changes across the magnitude of the independent variable.} 
\\\\
Unlike OLS which minimises the sum of the squared residuals, QR minimises the sum of the weighted absolute residuals. Therefore, for the $\tau$-th quantile $(0 < \tau < 1)$ the QR model tries to find the coefficient vector $\beta$ such that: $\hat{y}_{i} = X_{i}\beta$. This minimises the objective function:
\begin{align}
    \min_{\beta} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \rho_\tau(y_i - X_i\beta)
\end{align}
where $\rho_\tau(u)$ is the check-function defined as:
\begin{align}
    \rho_\tau(u) = \begin{cases} 
    \tau u & \text{if } u \geq 0 \\
    (\tau - 1) u & \text{if } u < 0 
    \end{cases}
\end{align}
As QR minimises the $\ell_1$ norm, all errors are treated with equal magnitude, unlike the $\ell_2$ norm in OLS.


\subsubsection{Elastic Net Regression}

The elastic net (EL) model blends both $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ norms for regularisation \cite{ZouHastie2005}. The $\ell_1$ norm, taken from the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO), adds a penalty term to the loss function proportional to the absolute value of the coefficients. This penalty term encourages model sparsity, driving some coefficients to zero; this effectively performs variable selection by excluding irrelevant variables. 
\\\\
The presence of the $\ell_2$ norm regularisation, as in Ridge regression, discourages large coefficients and tends to shrink them towards zero. However, unlike LASSO, it doesn't make them exactly zero and so all features remain in the model. 
\\\\
There are two important parameters in the EL. First, $\alpha$ controls the strength of the regularisation; setting $\alpha=0$ is equivalent to solving with OLS. Second, $\lambda$ acts as the mixing parameter and controls to what extent the EL is a mixture of LASSO and Ridge. Accordingly, the optimal $\beta$ is found through minimising the loss function:
\begin{align}
    \min_{\beta} \left\{ \frac{1}{2n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( y_i - X_i\beta \right)^2 + \alpha \left( \lambda \|\beta\|_1 + \frac{1-\lambda}{2} \|\beta\|_2^2 \right) \right\}
\end{align}

The advantages of the EL model are that, first, it is flexible because adjusting the $\lambda$ parameter can make the transition between Ridge and LASSO easy according to the needs of each dataset. Second, it tends to select groups of correlated variables together which helps mitigate instability. Third, it is particularly useful when dealing with highly correlated features as flagged earlier in Section \ref{sec:ChoosingFeatureSet}. 

\subsection{Machine learning models}

So far, all the models considered have been linear. The use of ML models allows me to capture non-linearities in the data. In theory, this might improve predictive power, but previous studies have shown that the gains using the latest ML models may be modest \cite{nguyen2021, Nguyenetal2023}. 

\subsubsection{Multi-layer Perceptron}

As the name suggests, the multi-layer perceptron (MLP) model is a type of artificial neural network that is composed of perceptrons stacked into layers. The leftmost layer, known as the input layer, consists of a set of neurons representing the input features. The rightmost layer is the output layer. The layers in between are known as hidden layers which take inputs from the previous layer, process them, and pass them on to the next layer. 
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
	\begin{subfigure}[t]{0.3\textwidth}
		\centering
	      \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{CarbonForecaster/report/figures/perceptron.png}
        \caption{Artificial neuron}
	      \label{fig:ArtificialNeuron}
	\end{subfigure}
	\hspace{0.15\textwidth}
	\begin{subfigure}[t]{0.3\textwidth}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{CarbonForecaster/report/figures/MLP.drawio.png}
        \caption{A two-layer fully-connected MLP}
		\label{fig:TwoLayerMLP}
	\end{subfigure}
 \caption{Stylised depiction of MLP structure}
 \fnote{Source: Adapted from Imperial College London's Introduction to Machine Learning, Lecture 4 notes \cite{CullyReiWang2023}}
\end{figure}
Put more formally, MLP is a supervised learning algorithm that learns a function $f:R^{m} \rightarrow R^{n}$ by training on a dataset where $m$ is the number of features and $n$ is the dimension of the output. Therefore, given the target vector $y$ and the feature vector $x=x_{1}, x_{2}, \dots , x_{m}$, it can learn a non-linear approximator for regression. The non-linearities are introduced through the activation functions. Using a non-linear activation function $\sigma$, a two-layer MLP is given by:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \mathbf{z}^{(1)} &= \sigma(\mathbf{W}^{(1)} \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^{(1)}) \\
    \mathbf{z}^{(2)} &= \sigma(\mathbf{W}^{(2)} \mathbf{z}^{(1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(2)}) \\
    \mathbf{y} &= \mathbf{W}^{(3)} \mathbf{z}^{(2)} + \mathbf{b}^{(3)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{W}^{(1)}\) is the weight matrix connecting the input layer to the first hidden layer and \(\mathbf{z}^{(1)}\) is the output of the first layer etc. For regression applications there is no activation in the output layer. 
\\\\
The weights and biases are learnt through backpropagation i.e. gradient descent applied to a computational graph. It iteratively applies the chain rule of calculus to propagate the error backward through the network layer by layer. For a loss function $L$ and a learning rate $\eta$, the weights are updated via:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{W}^{(l)} \leftarrow \mathbf{W}^{(l)} - \eta \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W}^{(l)}}
\end{align}

Through the use of hidden layers, the MLP can learn different layers of abstraction in the data through complex non-linear relationships. This is a key advantage over the naive and statistical models.  

\subsubsection{K Nearest-Neighbours}

K Nearest-Neighbours (KNN) is a non-parametric approach that makes predictions based on the closest available data points in the feature space \cite{SilvermanJones1989}. Using a distance measure, it finds a data point's closest neighbours in the training data and returns an average value for the target variable. As KNN doesn't assume any functional form between the features and the target, instead using only observation similarity, KNN inherently captures non-linear relationships. It has other advantages too: it is easy to implement, understand, and interpret. 
\\\\
Unfortunately, KNN exhibits the \textit{curse of dimensionality}: as KNN relies on distance metrics, it may struggle when modelling many features in high dimensional spaces. Specifically, if many features are irrelevant, instances that should be near to each other may instead be far away. Notwithstanding this problem, it remains a useful alternative regression approach due to its non-parametric nature.
\\\\
Regarding hyperparameters, the performance of KNN heavily depends on the choice of $k$, i.e. how many neighbours are taken into account. I optimise the choice using cross-validation, testing $k$ values from 5 to 500 with intervals of 5. 
\\\\
Another key choice is which distance measure to use. I try three measures: for two points $\mathbf{x} = (x_{1}, \dots, x_{n})$ and $\mathbf{y} = (y_{1}, \dots, y_{n})$: the Euclidian distance is given by $d_{\text{Euclidean}}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - y_i)^2}$. The Manhattan distance is given by $d_{\text{Manhattan}}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i - y_i|$. Finally, the Minkowski distance with parameter $p$ is $d_{\text{Minkowski}}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \left( \sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i - y_i|^p \right)^{\frac{1}{p}}.$ 


\subsubsection{XGBoost}

In the only two previous studies that have employed ML techniques to estimate GHG emissions, tree-based methods appear to have shown the most promise \cite{nguyen2021, Nguyenetal2023}. Moreover, decision trees are scale invariant, robust to irrelevant features and are readily interpretable. Therefore, the last two models I include in my set are both tree-based: XGBoost and Random Forest. 
\\\\
Gradient boosting is an iterative ensemble method where the models are built in sequence, each one correcting the errors of its predecessor. It does this by fitting a decision tree to the residuals (errors) of the previous model and then adding this tree to the ensemble. Through this process, gradient boosting gradually builds a strong predictive model by combining the outputs of multiple decision trees. This effectively captures complex interactions between features and handles non-linear relationships in the data.

	\centering

\end{figure}

Extreme gradient boosting (XGB) implements gradient boosting with enhancements: it includes $\ell_{1}$ and $\ell_{2}$ regularisation to prevent overfitting, uses an efficient splitting algorithm, and also employs second-order gradient optimisation. It is widely used by data scientists as a popular tool for ML problems \cite{ChenGuestrin2016}. 

\subsubsection{Random Forest}

A Random Forest (RF) is an ensemble learning method that builds a large number of decision trees during the training process and then combines their outputs using a majority vote to make a prediction. By using voting and ensuring diverse trees through randomness, RFs mitigate the risk of overfitting, which is a common issue with more simple decision trees \cite{Breiman2001}. The randomness in an RF is twofold. Firstly, RFs use ``bagging'' where each decision tree in the training phase is built on a different random data subset. This helps make the trees more diverse to reduce overfitting. In addition to randomly selecting only some rows, RFs also randomly sample from the features at each split in the decision trees. This allows different trees to focus on varying aspects of the data, again helping ensure diversity and prevent overfitting, which is the key disadvantage of decision trees. \\

\subsubsection{Measuring model performance}\label{sec:MeasuringModelPerformance}

Regarding performance measures, I largely focus on absolute percentage errors. I care about absolute errors as I value under- and overestimates symmetrically. Using percentage errors is helpful because they are easily interpretable and they normalise the error. 
\\\\
The primary metric I use to measure model prediction performance is the median absolute percentage error (MDAPE).

\begin{align}
    \text{MDAPE} = \text{median} \left( \left| \frac{y_1 - \hat{y}_1}{y_1} \right| \times 100, \left| \frac{y_2 - \hat{y}_2}{y_2} \right| \times 100, \dots, \left| \frac{y_n - \hat{y}_n}{y_n} \right| \times 100 \right)
\end{align}

I use MDAPE because it is a robust way to assess percentage error, being less sensitive than the mean. Nevertheless, the spread of estimated errors are also important, and so I also measure the interquartile range (IQR) of the absolute percentage errors (APE). 

\begin{align}
    \text{IQR(APE)} = Q_3 \left( \left| \frac{y_i - \hat{y}_i}{y_i} \right| \times 100 \right) - Q_1 \left( \left| \frac{y_i - \hat{y}_i}{y_i} \right| \times 100 \right)
\end{align}

where $Q_{3}$ is the third quartile (75th percentile) of the absolute percentage errors, and $Q_{1}$ is the first quartile (25th percentile) of the absolute percentage errors. 
\\\\
Following Nguyen et al. \cite{Nguyenetal2023}, I also measure the proportion of estimates that lie within $\pm50\%$
of the true value. In accordance with their terminology, I refer to this measure as the ``proportion of prediction in acceptance range'' (PPAR):
\begin{align}
    \text{PPAR} = \frac{1}{n} \times \sum \left( 1 \text{ if } \frac{y_i - \hat{y}_i}{y_i} \leq 50\%, 0 \text{ if otherwise } \right) \times 100
\end{align}
The final metric that may be of potential interest is the model's $R^{2}$. This measures how well the model fits the data, providing the ratio of variance in the target that is explained by the dependent variables. 

\begin{align}
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \bar{y})^2}
\end{align}

Despite its name, the $R^{2}$ can be negative if the model performs worse than simply predicting the mean of the target for all observations. This happens if the residual sum of squares is larger than the total sum of squares (the variance of the data around the mean). In other words, the model would perform worse than a horizontal line (representing the mean) for predicting the target.

Together, this suggest that LMC is among the most advanced reporters for its peer group. Therefore, it is possible that the model performs poorly because LMC is doing relatively better than what is to be expected of firms with a similar profile.

Firm B is Mitsubishi Corp (MC), a Japan-based company mainly engaged in living, mobility infrastructure, energy, electric power and others industrial fields. It is a very large conglomerate comprising ten business segments. As with LMC, my XGB model significantly overestimates the reported S3 emission intensity for MC. To understand the numbers in more detail, Figure \ref{fig:FirmBCatEmissions} shows the raw data loaded from the API for MC's category emissions. Here, the issue appears to stem from methodological changes resulting in volatile reporting.

Before 2019, no data is reported. Then, the years 2019-2021 provide a breakdown of the three points highlighted on Figure \ref{fig:XGBvsOLSPredictions}. The drop from 2019 is largely due to a reduction in reported category 11 emissions. 
\\\\
The years 2022 and 2023 show a dramatic increase in S3 emissions, but this data is not incorporated into my model. The year 2023 is out of scope as, due to reporting lags, much of the data is yet to come in for other firms. To prevent modelling 2023 on a partial sample, I choose to only model up to the year 2022.
\\\\
Regarding MC's 2022 numbers, the downstream emissions data is actually already winsorized by LSEG's internal processing rather than representing the true reported value from the firm. As my model only takes into account reported values directly from firms, MC's downstream categories are masked; as this includes category 11 which is a material category for MC's peer group, this results in MC containing no data for my model in 2022, and so Figure \ref{fig:XGBvsOLSPredictions} only shows three data points. Were the 2022 and 2023 to be plotted on Figure \ref{fig:XGBvsOLSPredictions}, the points would sit very close to the +100\% error line, with MC going from an overestimate to an underestimate. \\\\
Earlier in Section \ref{sec:Scope3Challenges}, I discussed how issues in the data generation process led to knock-on problems with the data; here, we see how MC provides a real-world example of reporting inconsistency. In MC's 2022 Sustainability Report, they report ``following GHG emission reduction targets announced in FY2021, [MC] calculates GHG emissions based on the GHG Protocol's equity share approach from the year ended March 2022'' \cite{MC2022}. 
\\\\
To collect the evidence together, first, it appears that before 2019, MC didn't report at all. The years 2019-2021 represent a partial disclosure as the change in category 11 in these years suggests a change in method. Years 2022 onwards then represent another change to methodology using the GHG Protocol's equity share approach. Together, this results in highly volatile data that is difficult to compare year-on-year. Therefore, it is perhaps unsurprising that even my best model yields large prediction errors for MC.

In contrast to LMC and MC, Firm C's predicted S3 intensity is significantly below its reported S3 intensity. Firm C is Fluidra SA (FSA), a Spain-based company active in the pool and wellness equipment business; its business sector (peer group) is cyclical consumer products. Figure \ref{fig:FirmCCatintensity} situates FSA's total S3 intensity against the other firms in its peer group.

	\centering

As with MC, FSA exhibits activity exclusion. In 2018 and 2019 I have no downstream categorical data. In my code this sets downstream emissions to zero, and so the 2018 and 2019 data points simply reflect the total reported upstream emission intensity. However, this is effectively dealt with using material emissions only: the materiality mask removes these data points from the modelling process, ensuring I don't train models on under-reported data such as these. 
\\\\
From 2020 onwards, a more complete picture of S3 categorical data emerges, and as is shown by Figure \ref{fig:FirmCCatintensity} FSA consistently reports the largest S3 emission intensity within its peer group. At least part of the explanation might be in its business: heated pools are among the most energy-intensive consumer products on the market.  
\\\\
However, another reason relates to the boundary incompleteness and difficulties with pulling together accurate data throughout the supply chain. For example, FSA operates in over 45 countries and this has had knock-on methodological impacts. According to their annual report, while beforehand they used only one emissions factor, ``in 2021  the IEA emission factor corresponding to the country of sale of the product has been considered.'' They add that  ``looking  ahead to 2022, [FSA] will continue to integrate the emissions associated with the energy consumption of its products during their useful life, until the entire catalog is covered'' \cite{Fluidra2022}. Therefore, from 2021 FSA moves from one emission factor to dozens, as well as expanding the catalogue of products included in its reporting. In other words, what they report and how evolves significantly.  
\\\\
For the 2022 data, FSA explains the continued high variation in their underlying S3 data from sources such as: ``an increase or decrease in the corresponding activity (as is the case for categories 2 and 5), an increase in the scope of the information collected (categories 2, 4, 6, 9 and 11), or the integration of methodological adjustments (or new emission factors) in their calculation (categories 1, 2, 3, 7, and 9)'' \cite{Fluidra2023}. In this case, when considering the variation in underlying data, it is difficult to disentangle the ``truth'' (changes in corresponding activity) from the data quality or coverage, especially with an aggregated target variable such as total S3 intensity.  
\\\\
This outlier analysis provides real-world instances of the potential issues discussed earlier in Section \ref{sec:DataGenIssues}. While activity exclusion can largely be dealt with using a material categories filter, for some firms the model may struggle due to reporting inconsistency and/or boundary incompleteness. This is a useful reminder that I am modelling a wide portfolio of firms that vary in their S3 reporting sophistication. Some firms only partially report and are screened out by materiality. However, even for those reporting material categories, methodologies can often begin crudely and improve over time, along with more refined data collection practices.\footnote{Incorporating methodological information will be discussed further in Section \ref{sec:IncorporatingMethodology}.} 
\\\\
Furthermore, for some firms, the model may struggle if they are at the frontier of S3 efforts, not only providing detailed and third-party verified estimates, but also actively engaging with suppliers and undertaking meaningful initiatives to reduce their carbon footprint. 
\\\\
In each case, the model nevertheless provides a useful first step. Where predictions deviate far from the reported values (visualised using the perpendicular distance from the red line in Figure \ref{fig:XGBS3TotalPredictions}), as these examples illustrate, this may reflect how far along the firm is in their S3 reporting methodology.

\section{The precautionary principle}\label{sec:PrecuationaryPrinciple}

The precautionary principle concerns how policy-makers act in high-stakes settings with large uncertainties. In simple terms, it tries to deter inaction due to uncertainty when such inaction has potentially catastrophic and irreversible consequences. It is often invoked in the areas of human health and the environment. For example, the precautionary principle might suggest that just because there is high uncertainty in climate projections, this doesn't imply that doing nothing is the best approach. 
\\\\
In the context of carbon reduction targets, it remains an open question as to how to apply the precautionary principle. The key problem is that while there are binding targets, many firms do not report their emissions. Therefore, how can one undertake S3 estimation given the need to incorporate and overcome this uncertainty? 
\\\\
In their paper, Hoepner and Rogelj \cite{Hoepner2021} argue that researchers need to assume that non-reporting firms or under-reporters are worst-in-class. As a consequence, their GHG emissions should be consistently and precautionarily overestimated at the 95th, 99th, or 99.9th percentile of their relevant peer group. The main aim of their approach is to incentivise non-reporters to disclose and encourage under-reporters to improve their accuracy.
\\\\
As a simple exercise, I compare the median S3 total intensity to the 95th percentile for the firms in my sample. If I group by their economic sector, the ratio is 8.3 (i.e. the 95th percentile amount is 8.3 times the reported median), and if I group by their business sector (one level down) the ratio is 8.9. Therefore, were I to apply this to non-reporting firms, I would be estimating GHG emission intensities of between 8 and 9 times the peer group median on average. 
\\\\
While such an approach would penalise non-reporters and perhaps incentivise them to disclose, as noted by Olesiewicz and Kooroshy \cite{olesiewicz2023} this approach is impractical for corporate emissions estimates, given the number of non-reporting companies, high degrees of firm heterogeneity and unrealistically high estimates which also tend to be volatile year-on-year.
\\\\
My framework offers an alternative way. As mentioned previously in Section \ref{sec:Observations}, by training my models on material S3 emissions I guard against the under-reporters influencing the models' learning. Using a model trained in this way, Figure \ref{fig:NonMaterialPredictions} shows how this can apply to non-material observations. Specifically, I divide my dataset into material and non-material observations according to their business sector. Next, I train an XGB model using material observations, and I make predictions on only the non-material observations.
\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
\includegraphics{CarbonForecaster/report/figures/s3_cat_material_predictions.png}
\caption{Predicted S3 intensity for non-material observations}
	\label{fig:NonMaterialPredictions}
 \fnote{Note: XGB model trained on material S3 observations and fitted to non-material S3 observations above.}
\end{figure}
From Figure \ref{fig:NonMaterialPredictions} it is clear to see that in most cases, fitting non-material observations by a model trained on material observations results in reported intensities below their predicted value, as one might intuit. Nevertheless, there are also some points above the red-dashed line, i.e. their reported values are higher than their predicted (material) values. This is a useful reminder that materiality does a very good job \textit{on average} of guarding against under-reporting, but it is not perfect for every firm.
\\\\
A precautionary principle applied to these under-reporters could be the maximum of either their reported S3 total intensity, or their predicted S3 total intensity based on the model trained on material observations. This approach both guards against under-reporting while also respecting the cases where reported values are above what would be expected. As required, this would provide a consistent precautionary approach but without estimating values that are unrealistically high.

However, the explanatory power of the models is considerably higher, and the best-performing model is now the simple baseline IFM rather than the tree-based methods. This supports my earlier conclusion that, unless there is a step-change to the data quality and coverage of reported S3 emissions, the utility of ML methods over simple baselines is likely to be limited.

\chapter{Potential future research}\label{Chapter:FutureApplications}

Following the results given in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Results}, one might be tempted to conclude that the use of data science, AI and ML in the context of S3 emissions estimation is of dubious benefit. However, just because ML methods currently struggle to accurately predict S3 emissions, there remain promising avenues to explore. In this Chapter, I briefly set out a few examples which I think look encouraging.

\section{Data collection, automation and interpolation}\label{sec:FurtherApplication1}

To focus the scope of this report, I have treated the categorical S3 reported data extracted from LSEG as my ground truth, and performed very limited interpolation on other explanatory variables (see Section \ref{sec:SampleConstruction} for details). This has the advantage of only using data directly reported by firms, but the drawback that it significantly reduces sample size given how many firms don't report at all. It may also introduce selection bias into the sample if certain types of firms (e.g. SMEs) are more likely under-report. 
\\\\
In a recent paper by Cullen, Marinoni and Cullen  \cite{CullenMarinoniCullen2024}, to tackle the problem of incompleteness in GHG datasets arising from both inconsistent reporting and poor transparency, the authors try a range of ML methods to automate the completion of GHG datasets. They explore three gap-filling tasks of increasing complexity, with the aim of correctly classifying emitters into four buckets ranging from low to high. Like my approach, their techniques also display a range of complexity, including simple interpolation, shallow learning models, ensemble models, deep learning models, and graph representation learning models. 
\\\\
The authors find that for simple gap-filling problems and datasets with fewer input features, simple models outperform complicated models in output average accuracy. However, as the gap-filling problems become more complex and datasets comprise an increasing number of input features from which models can learn, more complex deep-learning models output the best average accuracy scores.
\\\\
Cullen, Marinoni and Cullen show that while ML approaches are not the best way to automate the solution in every case, learning-based classifiers can sometimes be used to effectively and scalably complete emissions datasets.\footnote{The authors acknowledge that there are limitations to their approach: their results are based on a four-class classification which is too imprecise for many applications, and uncertainty isn't quantified. They aim to address these shortcomings in future work.} This helps address a key drawback to manual data-collection which is expensive, lacks comparison over different methodologies, and can be subject to human error.

\section{Improving supply chain visibility}\label{sec:FurtherApplication2}

Another promising collection of research uses ML techniques to combine datasets together for new applications. In this Section, I refer to two recent papers which employ ML to help build data across the supply chain with a view of improving data availability for researchers, firms, and investors interested in S3 estimation. 
\\\\
First, Jain et al. \cite{jain2023} estimate S3 emissions by classifying transaction descriptions of purchased goods and services into the US Environmentally-Extended Input-Output (USEEIO) commodities. They can then map these transactions onto commodities purchased and, by using the appropriate emissions factors, come to an estimated carbon footprint. By using fine-tuned natural language processing (NLP) models, the authors demonstrate that new insights can be gained by intelligently combining two previously unrelated datasets. 
\\\\
Another example of a novel bottom-up approach is in a paper by Mejia and Kajikawa \cite{Mejia2024} who employ network analysis to estimate investment-associated S3 emissions. By building a shareholder network of Japanese public firms, they are able to allocate and estimate S3 emissions arising from investments. 
\\\\
What is common to both of these papers is the use of ML to improve visibility of S3 emissions sources throughout the value chain. They represent ways to disentangle complex interactions in the supply chain which can sometimes cloud the availability or reliability of S3 data. 

\section{Incorporating methodology}\label{sec:IncorporatingMethodology}

From Section \ref{sec:OutlierAnalysis}, it is clear that accounting for the reporting methodology of firms is a crucial step to interpreting predictions (and, in my case, model outliers). As discussed earlier in Section \ref{sec:DataGenIssues}, the latitude that the GHG Protocol affords firms means that, when aggregating up across firms (or even comparing within firms across the years), figures are often incomparable without qualification.
\\\\
Accordingly, a useful approach would be to incorporate, perhaps as metadata, some key parameters about how the firm calculated their estimated S3 emissions. Such information might include the base year, key assumptions, whether they adopt an ownership or control approach, how each category has been modelled, and so on.  
\\\\
The potential of this information is best-illustrated by considering the materiality mask. This has proven an effective strategy for removing lower quality observations, enhancing the predictive accuracy of models and implementing the precautionary principle. This has been straightforward to implement using simple rules to check which categories are reported or not. It would be a more difficult exercise to use large language models (LLMs) with retreival augmented generation (RAG) to tag the data with methodological details. However, doing so would likely add useful colour to data quality and, in a similar way to the materiality mask, give the analyst the ability to experiment with filtering observation subsets for determining the best trade-off between data coverage and quality. 
\\\\
An example of such an application could be to tag the reported values according to whether they have been compiled in accordance with the GHG Protocol or the CDP questionnaire. This would ensure that models could then discriminate between different methodological frameworks.
\\\\
Estimating S3 emissions remains a promising field for applied ML. Following my analysis presented in the report, I believe that significant gains in predicting S3 emissions will be reliant on improved data coverage and quality, rather than in the elusive promise of a better model architecture. Accordingly, in this Chapter I have set out three avenues - automating interpolation, ML-generated datasets, and LLM RAG of methodologies - which may prove fertile ground for future progress.

\chapter{Conclusion}\label{Chapter:Conclusion}

Given the environmental implications of S3, and the legal and social imperative for firms to reduce their carbon footprints, estimating corporate GHG emissions is an ideal topic for research. Nevertheless, the nature of S3 emissions means that data is scarce, costly to collect, and of varying quality. Thus, the combination of importance and difficulty makes this an exciting area of applied data science.
\\\\
As with the previous study by Nguyen et al. \cite{Nguyenetal2023}, this paper has outlined the key data challenges and experimented with a range of models to predict S3 emissions. While caution should be taken when comparing results due to differing samples, my findings provide superior predictive performance. However, the main contribution of this work is not in demonstrating model superiority but in illustrating how issues with the underlying data—such as data coverage and quality—impact the model performance.
\\\\
Through a systematic, experimental approach, I have shown how key choices around the target variable, feature selection, observation sets, and model architecture shape prediction effectiveness. Selecting emission intensities and a core set of features, combined with improving data quality by focusing on material disclosures have proven to be essential for enhancing predictive accuracy. 
\\\\
In addition, the applications in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Extensions} provide further insights. By using only material observations, the issue of activity exclusion is mitigated, resulting in a more meaningful prediction target and the implementation of the precautionary principle. This represents a novel contribution to the field. The examples in this Chapter also underline the importance of carefully referring to the methodological information published alongside the raw data.
\\\\
To conclude, this paper has primarily comprised a regression exercise focused on predicting S3 emissions. Alongside Nguyen et al. \cite{Nguyenetal2023}, I find that ML approaches offer limited improvements over simpler methods such as IFM or OLS, especially given the trade-offs in terms of explainability. Nevertheless, careful data work has the potential to improve predictions and account for why models may struggle. In particular, Chapter \ref{Chapter:Extensions} ties together how the data problems anticipated in Chapter \ref{Chapter:Background} can help explain the results from Chapter \ref{Chapter:Results}. Furthermore, in Chapter \ref{Chapter:FutureApplications}, I have outlined several promising avenues where ML could further address the persistent issues of data quality and coverage. Ultimately, my findings suggest that the future of S3 estimation lies in improving data collection and reporting practices rather than refining model architecture.
